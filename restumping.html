Plain English

What is restumping?

Many Victorian homes sit on stumps under the floor. Over time, old timber stumps can rot, move, sink, or stop supporting the house evenly. Restumping means replacing those old stumps with stronger concrete or steel stumps.

Reblocking is the Victorian term for the same kind of work. Relevelling means adjusting the supports under the house to bring uneven floors back closer to level.

This is not yard clearing.

Richbright restumping is under-house foundation work. It is about the support structure below your floors, not removing tree stumps from a garden.

Signs to check

Your house may be telling you something.

These signs do not always mean a full restump is needed, but they are worth assessing before the problem gets worse.

Sloping floors

Floors that run downhill, bounce, or feel uneven can point to movement underneath.

Cracked walls

New or widening cracks may be linked to uneven support or house movement.

Sticking doors

Doors and windows that jam can be a sign the frame is shifting out of square.

Why it matters

Victorian homes need solid support underneath.

Older homes around Frankston and nearby suburbs can have timber stumps that have done decades of work. When the base moves, the rest of the house can show it through floors, walls, doors, and windows. A proper call-out gives you a clearer picture of what is happening under the floor before small warning signs turn into bigger repair work.
